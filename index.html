The Post-Mortem: How We Found This & How to Stop It

What you just watched scroll by is your digital footprint. And frankly, it's a fucking privacy shitshow. When you browse the web, you don't just ask for a webpage—your browser practically hands over your system's entire goddamn dossier in exchange. Advertising networks, security companies, and data brokers don't rely entirely on cookies anymore. That's old bullshit. Instead, they look at the exact combination of hardware, network settings, and system configurations you just exposed. Together, these form a "fingerprint" that is almost certainly unique to you.

Here is a breakdown of exactly how this script tricked your browser into coughing up that information, and more importantly, how you can lock this damn thing down.

1. VPN / Proxy Detection Heuristics

How we got it: Identifying a VPN solely from the browser is a bitch, but we use logic to catch you slipping. We checked the local timezone of your operating system (via your browser's system clock) and compared it to the geographical timezone associated with your IP address. If you are physically sitting in Dhaka but your IP claims you're in New York, the timezones mismatch, immediately flagging you as using a proxy. We also scan your Internet Service Provider (ISP) name for known datacenter hosts (like AWS, DigitalOcean, or M247) because regular residential users don't use server farms for casual browsing.

How to prevent it: Spoofing your system timezone to match your VPN's server timezone helps defeat the clock check. Extensions like Spoof Timezone can automate this. However, evading commercial database IP checks requires using Residential Proxies, which route traffic through actual hacked or leased household connections rather than data centers.

2. The Canvas Fingerprint

How we got it: HTML5 includes a powerful drawing tool called a <canvas>. Websites normally use this to render browser games or charts. In this script, we created an invisible canvas and forced your browser to draw a very specific, layered image using different colors and text fonts. Because every computer has a slightly different combination of graphics drivers, installed fonts, and sub-pixel smoothing algorithms, the resulting image is microscopically unique. We took a snapshot of those invisible pixels and hashed it into a unique ID code.

How to prevent it: Traditional ad-blockers like uBlock Origin usually don't stop this because drawing on a canvas is a legitimate web feature. To fight back, you need to use a browser that actually gives a damn, like Brave or the Tor Browser. If you use Firefox, install an extension called CanvasBlocker which silently injects fake, randomized data into the math every time a site tries to draw a hidden image, completely trashing their metrics.

3. AudioContext Fingerprinting

How we got it: We used the Web Audio API to create a high-frequency sine wave, pushed it through a digital audio compressor, and mathematically measured the result. We never actually played the sound through your speakers. Instead, we captured the digital audio buffer. Your specific sound card architecture dictates exactly how floating-point math is handled during processing. It's a ridiculously accurate, sneaky bastard of a hardware ID.

How to prevent it: Similar to canvas fingerprinting, you are fighting native web features. Using the Brave browser is currently one of the best defenses, as it intercepts audio API requests and injects cryptographic noise into the result, ruining their math. For absolute protection, you can completely disable JavaScript, though this will break almost the entire modern internet.

4. WebGL & GPU Extraction

How we got it: WebGL is the engine browsers use to render 3D graphics. We spun up a 3D environment in the background and simply asked it for the name of the hardware doing the heavy lifting by calling a specific debug extension (WEBGL_debug_renderer_info). Your browser happily handed over the exact make and model of your graphics card. Thanks for playing, sucker.

How to prevent it: You can disable WebGL entirely in your browser's advanced settings (in Firefox, type about:config and set webgl.disabled to true). Keep in mind that doing this will break browser-based 3D games, Google Earth, and advanced design tools.

5. Silent Media Device Enumeration

How we got it: You know how websites have to ask permission to use your camera and microphone? Yeah, well, they don't have to ask permission to just count them. By querying navigator.mediaDevices.enumerateDevices(), we asked your browser exactly how many audio inputs and video lenses are currently plugged into your machine. Even without granting us access to record, knowing you have exactly two webcams and three audio inputs adds another highly specific data point to your fingerprint.

How to prevent it: Safari actually restricts this by default until permission is granted, which is a rare Apple W. For Chrome and Firefox, you have to dig into deep configuration flags to randomize or spoof media enumeration. The easiest fix? Unplug your shit when you aren't using it.

6. Hardware & Network Telemetry

How we got it: We didn't even have to hack anything for this part—your browser just broadcasted it like a complete idiot. Using standard commands, we asked your machine how many CPU cores it has, how much RAM is installed, and what your screen's exact color-depth is. We then pinged a public IP lookup API to grab your location and ISP. When combined, this telemetry is easily enough to track you across different websites even if you religiously clear your cookies.

How to prevent it: The best defense against general telemetry is blending in with the crowd. The Tor Browser handles this exceptionally well by forcing every user to share the exact same screen size, hardware profile, and timezone, making it impossible for trackers to tell you apart from anyone else. For daily browsing, combining a strict privacy browser with a trusted VPN is your best bet to mask your IP and severely limit the amount of hardware data you leak.
