ABOUT TRACELINE

Look, the internet is fundamentally broken. TraceLine is a 100% open-source project built to show you exactly how much personal data you're bleeding every time you click a damn link. We aren't trying to sell you a VPN, and we don't save your data. We just want you to see the reality of modern browser fingerprinting. The source code is out there for anyone to audit.
SECURITY DISCLAIMER

Don't be an idiot. By clicking the button below, you are explicitly allowing this script to rip through your browser's internal APIs. It will extract your hardware identity, network footprint, rendering metrics, and whatever else it can grab to show you exactly what invisible trackers see.

Privacy Guarantee: There is no backend database connected to this page. Everything is processed locally, blasted onto your screen, and deleted the exact moment you close this tab.

The Post-Mortem: How We Found This & How to Stop It

What you just watched scroll by is your digital footprint. And frankly, it's a fucking privacy nightmare. When you browse the web, you don't just ask for a webpage—your browser practically hands over your system's entire goddamn dossier in exchange. Ad networks, security companies, and data brokers don't rely on cookies anymore. That's old bullshit. They look at the exact combination of hardware, network settings, and system configurations you just exposed.

Here is a breakdown of exactly how this script tricked your browser into coughing up that information, and more importantly, why some of it might be inaccurate (and why that's actually a good thing).

1. The "Inaccurate" Hardware Stats

Did the scan say you only have 8GB of RAM or 8 cores when you actually have way more? Good. Browsers like Firefox, Safari, and Brave intentionally lie to scripts like this. They cap the navigator.deviceMemory and hardwareConcurrency APIs. Why? Because if a script sees you have exactly 48GB of RAM and 14 cores, that makes you incredibly unique. By forcing everyone to look like they have generic 8GB/8-Core machines, you blend into the crowd. We attempted to pull the real data using Chromium's new Client Hints, but if you're heavily shielded, it gives us the middle finger.

2. Network Identification & The Adblock Test

How we got it: We hit multiple public IP APIs simultaneously. If your adblocker blocked the primary ones (like it did in your screenshot), we had a sneaky fallback ready (GeoJS) that almost never gets flagged. We also ran a two-part adblocker test: first, we created a fake invisible "ad" to see if your browser hid it, and second, we literally tried to ping a Google ad-tracking URL. If that network request got shot down, we know your shields are up.

How to protect it: Use a real, system-wide VPN. A browser proxy isn't always enough. And for adblocking, uBlock Origin is the undisputed king. Don't use the garbage built-in ones from standard Chrome.

3. The Canvas & Audio Fingerprints

How we got it: We created an invisible canvas and forced your graphic drivers to draw a tiny image. Every computer draws pixels microscopically differently based on OS, drivers, and fonts. We also generated a silent, high-frequency audio wave and measured how your specific sound card processed the floating-point math. These hashes are practically unique to your machine.

How to prevent it: Use a browser that fights back. Brave browser intercepts these requests and injects cryptographic noise into the math, ruining the fingerprint. If you use Firefox, get the CanvasBlocker extension immediately.

4. WebGL & GPU Extraction

How we got it: We spun up a 3D WebGL environment in the background and just... asked it what graphics card was doing the work. Your browser happily handed over the exact make and model. Thanks for playing, sucker.
